<p>The <b>Austrian Empire</b> (<a href="/wiki/Austrian_German_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Austrian German language">Austrian German</a>: <i lang="de-AT">Kaiserthum Oesterreich</i>, modern spelling <i><i lang="de" title="German language text">Kaisertum Österreich</i></i>) was a <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central European</a> <a href="/wiki/Multinational_state" title="Multinational state">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">great power</a> from 1804 to 1867, created by proclamation out of the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">realms of the Habsburgs</a>. During its existence, it was the third most populous <a href="/wiki/Empire" title="Empire">empire</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a> in Europe. Along with <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a>, it was one of the two major powers of the <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a>. Geographically, it was the third largest empire in Europe after the Russian Empire and the First French Empire (621,538 square kilometres; 239,977 sq mi). Proclaimed in response to the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">First French Empire</a>, it partially overlapped with the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> until the latter's dissolution in 1806.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary_(1526%E2%80%931867)" title="Kingdom of Hungary (1526–1867)">Kingdom of Hungary</a> — as Regnum Independens — was administered by its own institutions separately from the rest of the empire. After Austria was defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a> of 1866, the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Compromise_of_1867" title="Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867">Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867</a> was adopted, joining together the Kingdom of Hungary and the Empire of Austria to form <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austrian_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation">Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austrian_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Imperial_and_Royal_Army_during_the_Napoleonic_Wars" title="Imperial and Royal Army during the Napoleonic Wars">Imperial and Royal Army during the Napoleonic Wars</a></div>
<p>Changes shaping the nature of the Holy Roman Empire took place during conferences in <a href="/wiki/Rastatt" title="Rastatt">Rastatt</a> (1797–1799) and <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a> (1801–1803). On 24 March 1803, the <a href="/wiki/Reichsdeputationshauptschluss" title="Reichsdeputationshauptschluss">Imperial Recess</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Reichsdeputationshauptschluss</i>) was declared, which reduced the number of <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical state">ecclesiastical states</a> from 81 to only 3 and the <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">free imperial cities</a> from 51 to 6. This measure was aimed at replacing the old constitution of the Holy Roman Empire, but the actual consequence of the Imperial Recess was the end of the empire. Taking this significant change into consideration, the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a>  Francis II created the title <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Austria" title="Emperor of Austria">Emperor of Austria</a>, for himself and his successors.
</p><p>In 1804, the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> Francis II, who was also ruler of the lands of the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">Habsburg Monarchy</a>, founded the Empire of Austria, in which all his lands were included. In doing so he created a formal overarching structure for the Habsburg Monarchy, which had functioned as a <a href="/wiki/Composite_monarchy" title="Composite monarchy">composite monarchy</a> for about three hundred years. He did so because he foresaw either the end of the Holy Roman Empire, or the eventual accession as Holy Roman Emperor of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>, who had earlier that year adopted the title of an Emperor of the French; Francis II eventually abandoned the title of German-Roman Emperor later in 1806. To safeguard his dynasty's imperial status he adopted the additional hereditary title of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Austria" title="Emperor of Austria">Emperor of Austria</a>. Apart from now being included in a new "<a href="/wiki/Kaiserthum" title="Kaiserthum">Kaiserthum</a>", the workings of the overarching structure and the status of its component lands at first stayed much the same as they had been under the composite monarchy that existed before 1804.
</p><p>This was especially demonstrated by the status of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary_(1526%E2%80%931867)" title="Kingdom of Hungary (1526–1867)">Kingdom of Hungary</a>, a country that had never been a part of the Holy Roman Empire and which had always been considered a separate realm—a status that was affirmed by Article X, which was added to Hungary's constitution in 1790 during the phase of the composite monarchy and described the state as a <i>Regnum Independens</i>. Hungary's affairs remained administered by its own institutions (King and Diet) as they had been beforehand. Thus no Imperial institutions were involved in its government.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The fall and <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire">dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire</a> was accelerated by French intervention in the Empire in September 1805. On 20 October 1805, an Austrian army led by General <a href="/wiki/Karl_Mack_von_Leiberich" title="Karl Mack von Leiberich">Karl Mack von Leiberich</a> was <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ulm" title="Battle of Ulm">defeated by French armies near the town of Ulm</a>. The French victory resulted in the capture of 20,000 Austrian soldiers and many cannons. Napoleon's army won another victory at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Austerlitz" title="Battle of Austerlitz">Austerlitz</a> on 2 December 1805. Francis was forced into negotiations with the French from 4 to 6 December 1805, which concluded with an armistice on 6 December 1805.
</p><p>The French victories encouraged rulers of certain imperial territories to ally themselves with the French and assert their formal independence from the Empire. On 10 December 1805, <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_IV_Joseph" class="mw-redirect" title="Maximilian IV Joseph">Maximilian IV Joseph</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">prince-elector</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke of Bavaria">Duke of Bavaria</a>, proclaimed himself King, followed by the Duke of Württemberg <a href="/wiki/Frederick_III,_Duke_of_Wurttemberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick III, Duke of Wurttemberg">Frederick III</a> on 11 December. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Frederick,_Grand_Duke_of_Baden" title="Charles Frederick, Grand Duke of Baden">Charles Frederick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margrave_of_Baden" class="mw-redirect" title="Margrave of Baden">Margrave of Baden</a>, was given the title of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duke" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Duke">Grand Duke</a> on 12 December. Each of these new states became French allies. The Treaty of Pressburg between France and Austria, signed in Pressburg (today <a href="/wiki/Bratislava" title="Bratislava">Bratislava</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>) on 26 December, enlarged the territory of Napoleon's German allies at the expense of defeated Austria.
</p><p>Francis II agreed to the humiliating <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Pressburg_(1805)" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Pressburg (1805)">Treaty of Pressburg</a> (26 December 1805), which in practice meant the dissolution of the long-lived <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> and a reorganization under a Napoleonic imprint of the German territories lost in the process into a precursor state of what became <a href="/wiki/Germany#Modern_Germany" title="Germany">modern Germany</a>, those possessions nominally having been part of the Holy Roman Empire within the present boundaries of Germany, as well as other measures weakening Austria and the Habsburgs in other ways. Certain Austrian holdings in Germany were passed to French allies—the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Bavaria" title="King of Bavaria">King of Bavaria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/King_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Württemberg">King of Württemberg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Elector_of_Baden" class="mw-redirect" title="Elector of Baden">Elector of Baden</a>. Austrian claims on those German states were renounced without exception.
</p><p>On 12 July 1806, the <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_the_Rhine" title="Confederation of the Rhine">Confederation of the Rhine</a> was established, comprising 16 sovereigns and countries. This confederation, under French influence, put an end to the Holy Roman Empire. On 6 August 1806, even Francis recognized the new state of things and proclaimed the dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire, as he did not want Napoleon to succeed him. This action was unrecognized by <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">George III of the United Kingdom</a> who was also the <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Elector</a> of Hanover and had also lost his German territories around <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Electorate of Brunswick-Lüneburg">Hanover</a> to Napoleon. His claims were later settled by the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hanover" title="Kingdom of Hanover">Kingdom of Hanover</a> which was held by George's British heirs until <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>'s accession, when it split into the British and Hanoverian royal families.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metternich_era">Metternich era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austrian_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Metternich era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Declaration_of_victory_after_the_Battle_of_Leipzig,_1813_(by_Johann_Peter_Krafft).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Declaration_of_victory_after_the_Battle_of_Leipzig%2C_1813_%28by_Johann_Peter_Krafft%29.jpg/220px-Declaration_of_victory_after_the_Battle_of_Leipzig%2C_1813_%28by_Johann_Peter_Krafft%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Declaration_of_victory_after_the_Battle_of_Leipzig%2C_1813_%28by_Johann_Peter_Krafft%29.jpg/330px-Declaration_of_victory_after_the_Battle_of_Leipzig%2C_1813_%28by_Johann_Peter_Krafft%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Declaration_of_victory_after_the_Battle_of_Leipzig%2C_1813_%28by_Johann_Peter_Krafft%29.jpg/440px-Declaration_of_victory_after_the_Battle_of_Leipzig%2C_1813_%28by_Johann_Peter_Krafft%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="8021" data-file-height="5694" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Declaration_of_victory_after_the_Battle_of_Leipzig,_1813_(by_Johann_Peter_Krafft).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Karl_Philipp,_Prince_of_Schwarzenberg" title="Karl Philipp, Prince of Schwarzenberg">Karl von Schwarzenberg</a> and the monarchs of Austria, Prussia, and Russia after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Leipzig" title="Battle of Leipzig">Battle of Leipzig</a>, 1813</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Klemens_von_Metternich" title="Klemens von Metternich">Klemens von Metternich</a> became Foreign Minister in 1809. He also held the post of Chancellor of State from 1821 until 1848, under both <a href="/wiki/Francis_II_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Francis II of Austria">Francis II</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Austria" title="Ferdinand I of Austria">Ferdinand I</a>. The period of 1815-1848 is also referred to as the "<a href="/wiki/Age_of_Metternich" class="mw-redirect" title="Age of Metternich">Age of Metternich</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Sked,_Alan_1918_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sked,_Alan_1918-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> During this period, Metternich controlled the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">Habsburg Monarchy</a>'s foreign policy. He also had a major influence in European politics. He was known for his strong conservative views and approach in politics. Metternich's policies were strongly against revolution and liberalism.<sup id="cite_ref-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In his opinion, liberalism was a form of legalized revolution.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Metternich believed that <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a> was the only proper system of government.<sup id="cite_ref-Sked,_Alan_1918_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sked,_Alan_1918-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This notion influenced his anti-revolutionary policy to ensure the continuation of the Habsburg monarchy in Europe. Metternich was a practitioner of balance-of-power diplomacy.<sup id="cite_ref-Sofka,_James_R_1998_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sofka,_James_R_1998-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> His foreign policy aimed to maintain international political equilibrium to preserve the Habsburgs' power and influence in international affairs. Following the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, Metternich was the chief architect of the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> in 1815.<sup id="cite_ref-Sofka,_James_R_1998_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sofka,_James_R_1998-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The Austrian Empire was the main beneficiary from the Congress of Vienna and it established an alliance with <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> forming the <a href="/wiki/Quadruple_Alliance_(1815)" title="Quadruple Alliance (1815)">Quadruple Alliance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Austrian Empire also gained new territories from the Congress of Vienna, and its influence expanded to the north through the <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a> and also into <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Due to the Congress of Vienna in 1815, Austria was the leading member of the German Confederation.<sup id="cite_ref-Mueller-1961_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mueller-1961-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Following the Congress, the major European powers agreed to meet and discuss resolutions in the event of future disputes or revolutions. Because of Metternich's main role in the architecture of the Congress, these meetings are also referred to as the "Metternich congress" or "Metternich system". Under Metternich as the Austrian foreign minister, other congresses would meet to resolve European foreign affairs. These included the Congresses of Aix-la-Chapelle (1818), Carlsbad (1819), Troppau (1820), Laibach (1821), and Verona (1822).<sup id="cite_ref-Sked,_Alan_1918_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sked,_Alan_1918-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Metternich congresses aimed to maintain the political equilibrium among the European powers and prevent revolutionary efforts. These meetings also aimed to resolve foreign issues and disputes without resorting to violence. By means of these meetings and by allying the Austrian Empire with other European powers whose monarchs had a similar interest in preserving conservative political direction, Metternich was able to establish the Austrian Empire's influence on European politics. Also, because Metternich used the fear of revolutions among European powers, which he also shared, he was able to establish security and predominance of the Habsburgs in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Under Metternich, <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalist</a> revolts in Austrian north Italy and in the German states were forcibly crushed. At home, he pursued a similar policy to suppress revolutionary and liberal ideals. He employed the Carlsbad Decrees of 1819, which used strict <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">censorship</a> of education, press and speech to repress revolutionary and liberal concepts.<sup id="cite_ref-Sked,_Alan_1918_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sked,_Alan_1918-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Metternich also used a wide-ranging spy network to dampen down unrest.
</p><p>Metternich operated very freely with regard to foreign policy under Emperor Francis II's reign. Francis died in 1835. This date marks the decline of Metternich's influence in the Austrian Empire. Francis' heir was his son Ferdinand I, but he suffered from an intellectual disability.<sup id="cite_ref-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Ferdinand's accession preserved the Habsburg dynastic succession, but he was not capable of ruling.<sup id="cite_ref-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The leadership of the Austrian Empire was transferred to a state council composed of Metternich, Francis II's brother Archduke Louis, and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Anton_von_Kolowrat-Liebsteinsky" title="Franz Anton von Kolowrat-Liebsteinsky">Count Franz Anton Kolowrat</a>, who later became the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Ministers-President_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Ministers-President of Austria">first Minister-President</a> of the Austrian Empire. The liberal <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848_in_the_Austrian_Empire" title="Revolutions of 1848 in the Austrian Empire">Revolutions of 1848 in the Austrian Empire</a> forced Metternich's resignation. Metternich is remembered for his success in maintaining the status quo and the Habsburg influence in international affairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Sked,_Alan_1918_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sked,_Alan_1918-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> No Habsburg foreign minister following Metternich held a similar position within the empire for such a long time nor held such a vast influence on European foreign affairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jelavich,_Barbara_1918-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Historians often remember the Metternich era as a period of stagnation: the Austrian Empire fought no wars nor did it undergo any radical internal reforms.<sup id="cite_ref-Crankshaw,_Edward_1963_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crankshaw,_Edward_1963-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, it was also thought of as a period of economic growth and prosperity in the Austrian Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-Crankshaw,_Edward_1963_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crankshaw,_Edward_1963-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The population of Austria rose to 37.5 million by 1843. Urban expansion also occurred and the population of Vienna reached 400,000. During the Metternich era, the Austrian Empire also maintained a stable economy and reached an almost balanced budget, despite having a major deficit following the Napoleonic Wars.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revolutions_of_1848">Revolutions of 1848</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austrian_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Revolutions of 1848">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848_in_the_Austrian_Empire" title="Revolutions of 1848 in the Austrian Empire">Revolutions of 1848 in the Austrian Empire</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A_m%C3%A1sodik_kom%C3%A1romi_csatA_1849._j%C3%BAlius_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/A_m%C3%A1sodik_kom%C3%A1romi_csatA_1849._j%C3%BAlius_2.jpg/220px-A_m%C3%A1sodik_kom%C3%A1romi_csatA_1849._j%C3%BAlius_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/A_m%C3%A1sodik_kom%C3%A1romi_csatA_1849._j%C3%BAlius_2.jpg/330px-A_m%C3%A1sodik_kom%C3%A1romi_csatA_1849._j%C3%BAlius_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/A_m%C3%A1sodik_kom%C3%A1romi_csatA_1849._j%C3%BAlius_2.jpg/440px-A_m%C3%A1sodik_kom%C3%A1romi_csatA_1849._j%C3%BAlius_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3850" data-file-height="2629" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A_m%C3%A1sodik_kom%C3%A1romi_csatA_1849._j%C3%BAlius_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Kom%C3%A1rom_(1849)" title="Second Battle of Komárom (1849)">Battle of Komárom</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_Revolution_of_1848" title="Hungarian Revolution of 1848">Hungarian Revolution</a>, 1849</div></div></div>
<p>From March 1848 through November 1849, the Empire was threatened by revolutionary movements, most of which were of a nationalist character. Besides that, liberal and even socialist currents resisted the empire's longstanding conservatism. Although most of the revolution plans failed, some changes were made; significant lasting reforms included the abolition of <a href="/wiki/Serfdom" title="Serfdom">serfdom</a>, cancellation of <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">censorship</a> and a promise made by <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Austria" title="Ferdinand I of Austria">Ferdinand I of Austria</a> said to implement a <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> throughout the whole Empire.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Bach_years">The Bach years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austrian_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Bach years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the death of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Felix_of_Schwarzenberg" title="Prince Felix of Schwarzenberg">Prince Felix of Schwarzenberg</a> in 1852, the Minister of the Interior <a href="/wiki/Baron_Alexander_von_Bach" title="Baron Alexander von Bach">Baron Alexander von Bach</a> largely dictated policy in Austria and Hungary. Bach centralized administrative authority for the Austrian Empire, but he also endorsed <a href="/wiki/Reactionary" title="Reactionary">reactionary</a> policies that reduced <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press" title="Freedom of the press">freedom of the press</a> and abandoned public trials. He later represented the <a href="/wiki/Enlightened_absolutism" title="Enlightened absolutism">Absolutist</a> (or <i>Klerikalabsolutist</i>) direction, which culminated in the concordat of August 1855 that gave the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> control over education and family life. This period in the history of the Austrian Empire would become known as the era of neo-absolutism, or Bach's absolutism.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Francesco_Giuseppe_fra_le_truppe_a_Solferino_1859.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Francesco_Giuseppe_fra_le_truppe_a_Solferino_1859.jpg/220px-Francesco_Giuseppe_fra_le_truppe_a_Solferino_1859.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Francesco_Giuseppe_fra_le_truppe_a_Solferino_1859.jpg/330px-Francesco_Giuseppe_fra_le_truppe_a_Solferino_1859.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Francesco_Giuseppe_fra_le_truppe_a_Solferino_1859.jpg/440px-Francesco_Giuseppe_fra_le_truppe_a_Solferino_1859.jpg 2x" data-file-width="474" data-file-height="295" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Francesco_Giuseppe_fra_le_truppe_a_Solferino_1859.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Austrian Emperor <a href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_I_of_Austria" title="Franz Joseph I of Austria">Franz Joseph</a> with his troops at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Solferino" title="Battle of Solferino">Battle of Solferino</a>, 1859</div></div></div>
<p>The pillars of the so-called Bach system (<i>Bachsches System</i>) were, in the words of <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Fischhof" title="Adolf Fischhof">Adolf Fischhof</a>, four "armies": <i>a standing army of soldiers, a sitting army of office holders, a kneeling army of priests and a fawning army of sneaks</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Prisons were full of political prisoners, like <a href="/wiki/Czechs" title="Czechs">Czech</a> nationalist journalist and writer <a href="/wiki/Karel_Havl%C3%AD%C4%8Dek_Borovsk%C3%BD" title="Karel Havlíček Borovský">Karel Havlíček Borovský</a> who was forcibly expatriated  (1851–1855) to <a href="/wiki/Brixen" title="Brixen">Brixen</a>. This exile undermined Borovský's health and he died soon afterwards. This affair earned Bach a very bad reputation amongst Czechs and subsequently led to the strengthening of the <a href="/wiki/Czech_National_Revival" title="Czech National Revival">Czech national movement</a>.
</p><p>However, Bach's relaxed ideological views (apart from the neo-absolutism) led to a great rise in the 1850s of <a href="/wiki/Economic_freedom" title="Economic freedom">economic freedom</a>. Internal <a href="/wiki/Customs_(tax)" class="mw-redirect" title="Customs (tax)">customs</a> duties were abolished, and peasants were emancipated from their feudal obligations.<sup id="cite_ref-nie_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nie-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In her capacity as leader of the German Confederation, Austria participated with volunteers in the <a href="/wiki/First_War_of_Schleswig" class="mw-redirect" title="First War of Schleswig">First War of Schleswig</a> (1848–1850).<sup id="cite_ref-Mueller-1961_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mueller-1961-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sardinia" title="Kingdom of Sardinia">Sardinia</a> allied itself with <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> for the conquest of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Lombardy%E2%80%93Venetia" title="Kingdom of Lombardy–Venetia">Lombardy–Venetia</a>. Austria was defeated in the 1859 armed conflict. The Treaties of <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Villafranca" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Villafranca">Villafranca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Z%C3%BCrich" title="Treaty of Zürich">Zürich</a> removed Lombardy, except for the part east of the Mincio river, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Mantovano" title="Mantovano">Mantovano</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_1859">After 1859</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austrian_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: After 1859">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/February_Patent" title="February Patent">Constitution of 1861</a> created a House of Lords (<i lang="de" title="German language text">Herrenhaus</i>) and a House of Deputies (<i lang="de" title="German language text">Abgeordnetenhaus</i>). But most nationalities of the monarchy remained dissatisfied.
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Second_Schleswig_War" title="Second Schleswig War">second war</a> with Denmark in 1864, Holstein came under Austrian, Schleswig and Lauenburg under Prussian administration. But the internal difficulties continued.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Diets replaced the parliament in 17 provinces, the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Hungarian people">Hungarians</a> pressed for autonomy, and Venetia was attracted by the now unified Italy.
</p><p>After Austria was defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a> of 1866 and the German Confederation was dissolved, the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Compromise_of_1867" title="Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867">Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867</a> was adopted. By this act, the Kingdom of Hungary and the Empire of Austria as two separate entities joined together on an equal basis to form the Dual Monarchy of Austria-Hungary.
</p><p>The frequent abbreviation <a href="/wiki/K.u.K." class="mw-redirect" title="K.u.K.">K.u.K.</a> (<i>Kaiserliche und Königliche</i>, "Imperial and Royal") does not refer to that dual monarchy but originated in 1745, when the "royal" part referred to the Apostolic Kingdom of Hungary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_policy">Foreign policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Austrian_Empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Foreign policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vienna_Congress.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Vienna_Congress.jpg/220px-Vienna_Congress.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Vienna_Congress.jpg/330px-Vienna_Congress.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Vienna_Congress.jpg/440px-Vienna_Congress.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1452" data-file-height="923" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vienna_Congress.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Metternich alongside <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">Wellington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Maurice_de_Talleyrand-P%C3%A9rigord" title="Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord">Talleyrand</a>, and other European diplomats at the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a>, 1815</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> dominated Austrian foreign policy from 1804 to 1815. The Austrian army was one of the most formidable forces the French had to face. After <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> signed a peace treaty with France on 5 April 1795, Austria was forced to carry the main burden of war with <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">Napoleonic France</a> for almost ten years. This severely overburdened the Austrian economy, making the war greatly unpopular. Emperor Francis II therefore refused to join any further war against Napoleon for a long time. On the other hand, Francis II continued to intrigue for the possibility of revenge against France, entering into a secret military agreement with the Russian Empire in November 1804. This convention was to assure mutual cooperation in the case of a new war against France.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Austrian unwillingness to join the Third Coalition was overcome by British subsidies, but the Austrians withdrew from the war yet again after a decisive defeat at the Battle of Austerlitz. Although the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Budget" title="Budget">budget</a> suffered from wartime expenditures and its international position was significantly undermined, the humiliating Treaty of Pressburg provided plenty of time to strengthen the army and economy. Moreover, the ambitious <a href="/wiki/Archduke_Charles,_Duke_of_Teschen" title="Archduke Charles, Duke of Teschen">Archduke Charles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Philipp_von_Stadion" class="mw-redirect" title="Johann Philipp von Stadion">Johann Philipp von Stadion</a> never abandoned the goal of further war with France.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Austrian_Empire_(1812).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Austrian_Empire_%281812%29.svg/220px-Austrian_Empire_%281812%29.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Austrian_Empire_%281812%29.svg/330px-Austrian_Empire_%281812%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Austrian_Empire_%281812%29.svg/440px-Austrian_Empire_%281812%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Austrian_Empire_(1812).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Austrian Empire in 1812</div></div></div>
<p>Archduke Charles of Austria served as the Head of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_War_(Austria)" class="mw-redirect" title="Council of War (Austria)">Council of War</a> and Commander in Chief of the Austrian army. Endowed with the enlarged powers, he reformed the Austrian Army to preparedness for another war. Johann Philipp von Stadion, the foreign minister, personally hated Napoleon due to an experience of confiscation of his possessions in France by Napoleon. In addition, the third wife of Francis II, <a href="/wiki/Maria_Ludovika_of_Austria-Este" title="Maria Ludovika of Austria-Este">Maria Ludovika of Austria-Este</a>, agreed with Stadion's efforts to begin a new war. <a href="/wiki/Klemens_Wenzel_von_Metternich" class="mw-redirect" title="Klemens Wenzel von Metternich">Klemens Wenzel von Metternich</a>, located in Paris, called for careful advance in the case of the war against France. The defeat of French army at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bail%C3%A9n" title="Battle of Bailén">Battle of Bailén</a> in Spain on 27 July 1808 triggered the war. On 9 April 1809, an Austrian force of 170,000 men attacked <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Bavaria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite military defeats—especially the Battles of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Marengo" title="Battle of Marengo">Marengo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ulm" title="Battle of Ulm">Ulm</a>, Austerlitz and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Wagram" title="Battle of Wagram">Wagram</a>—and consequently lost territory throughout the Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars (the Treaties of <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Campo_Formio" title="Treaty of Campo Formio">Campo Formio</a> in 1797, <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Luneville" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Luneville">Luneville</a> in 1801, <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Pressburg_(1805)" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Pressburg (1805)">Pressburg</a> in 1806, and <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Sch%C3%B6nbrunn" title="Treaty of Schönbrunn">Schönbrunn</a> in 1809), Austria played a decisive part in the overthrow of Napoleon in the campaigns of 1813–14. It participated in a second invasion of France in 1815, and put an end to Murat's regime in south Italy.
</p><p>The latter period of Napoleonic Wars featured <a href="/wiki/Klemens_Wenzel,_Prince_von_Metternich" class="mw-redirect" title="Klemens Wenzel, Prince von Metternich">Metternich</a> exerting a large degree of influence over foreign policy in the Austrian Empire, a matter nominally decided by the Emperor. Metternich initially supported an alliance with France, arranging the marriage between Napoleon and Francis II's daughter, Marie-Louise; however, by the 1812 campaign, he had realised the inevitability of Napoleon's downfall and took Austria to war against France. Metternich's influence at the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> was remarkable, and he became not only the premier statesman in Europe but the virtual ruler of the Empire until 1848—the <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848" title="Revolutions of 1848">Year of Revolutions</a>—and the rise of <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberalism</a> equated to his political downfall. The result was that the Austrian Empire was seen as one of the great powers after 1815, but also as a reactionary force and an obstacle to national aspirations in Italy and Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During this time, Metternich was able to maintain an elaborate balance between Prussia, the lesser German states, and Austria in the <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a>. Thanks to his efforts, Austria was seen as the senior partner with Prussia keeping watch over Germany as a whole. Further, Metternich opposed the weakening of France in the years after Napoleon, and viewed the new monarchy in Paris as an effective tool in keeping Russia at bay. From 1815 to 1848, <a href="/wiki/Klemens_von_Metternich" title="Klemens von Metternich">Metternich</a> steered Austria Imperial foreign policy, and indeed the mood of Europe, and managed to keep peace on the continent despite the growing liberal and radical movements inside most major powers. His resignation in 1848, forced by moderates in the court, and <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848" title="Revolutions of 1848">revolutionaries</a> in the streets, may have caused the spread of the revolutions throughout the monarchy. It is stipulated that Metternich's departure emboldened liberal factions in Austria and Hungary, but this cannot be confirmed for certain.
</p>
